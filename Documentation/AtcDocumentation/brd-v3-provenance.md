## How This BRD Was Produced

BRD v3 was **dictated by a human to an LLM** — not generated by one. The distinction
matters.

Over approximately 12 hours of detailed design sessions, the human author directed
every requirement, every threshold, every scope boundary, and every design decision.
"Dictated" in the Julius Caesar sense — the human commanded, the LLM transcribed and
structured. The LLM organized the author's decisions into BRD format, but the
substance is entirely human-directed.

The BRD was then reviewed by the human author. Edits and refinements were further
dictated by the human through an iterative revision cycle (documented in the
BRD Revision Log). The final document was approved by the human author on 2026-02-28.

**This BRD was not hallucinated.** It was not "suggested" by an AI and rubber-stamped.
It was authored through adversarial, critical dialogue where the LLM's role was to
challenge, structure, and organize — not to invent requirements.

## Anticipated Skeptic Arguments

### "The fox designed the hen house."

The author of this BRD is also the technology owner of the ETL framework being
validated. A skeptic could argue this creates a conflict of interest — that the author
might leave deliberate gaps or back doors that favor the builder side.

**Response:** Show us the back door. The BRD is a complete document with 128 business
requirement IDs, 18 design decisions, and 13 revision decisions — all documented and
traceable. If the fox left a gap in the fence, cite the BR ID, point to the section,
and explain how it creates an exploitable weakness. The document is available for
adversarial review. Vague accusations of bias are not a substitute for specific
findings.

### "One person can't anticipate every edge case."

A valid concern in the abstract. Less valid in the specific.

**Response:** The author of this BRD owns the existing ETL framework's technology and
personally led two of the migration teams that moved thousands of ETL jobs from
on-prem to cloud. The design decisions in this BRD — hash-sort comparison, encoding
normalization, part-file reassembly, CSV dialect handling — are drawn directly from
hard-won operational experience, not theoretical architecture.

Does this person know every gotcha in the system? Maybe not. But the skeptic is
invited to point to literally anyone on the planet more qualified to write this
specification. The author has more hands-on migration comparison experience than
any vendor team that would be hired to build the production tool.

### "An LLM was involved, so it's tainted."

The LLM was a transcription and structuring tool. The requirements came from the
human. If the involvement of an LLM in document preparation is disqualifying, then
every document produced with spell-check, grammar tools, or dictation software is
equally suspect. The question is not "was a tool used?" but "who made the decisions?"

The answer: the human made every decision. The LLM challenged, organized, and
formatted. The revision log documents the decision chain.
